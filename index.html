4. Generator

Converts mechanical rotational energy into electrical energy through electromagnetic induction

‚Üì

5. Power Converter & Transformer

‚Üì

6. Output: Electrical Power to Grid

Clean electricity distributed to homes, industries, and commercial establishments

Detailed Explanation

Source of Energy: Wind energy originates from solar radiation heating Earth's surface unevenly, creating pressure differences that drive air movement. This kinetic energy in moving air masses serves as the primary energy source for wind power systems.

Conversion Process: The conversion begins when wind strikes the turbine blades, creating aerodynamic lift forces that rotate the rotor. This rotational mechanical energy passes through a gearbox that amplifies the rotational speed to match generator requirements. The generator then employs electromagnetic induction principles to transform mechanical rotation into alternating current electricity. Power electronics and transformers condition this electrical output to match grid specifications.

Output & Utilization: The system produces three-phase AC electrical power ranging from kilowatts for small turbines to several megawatts for large offshore installations. This electricity feeds directly into the power grid for distribution.

Real-World Application: Wind farms in India, particularly in Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, and Karnataka, contribute significantly to the national renewable energy portfolio. Offshore wind projects are gaining traction globally, providing consistent power generation due to stable marine wind patterns, supporting grid stability and reducing carbon emissions.

6. Output: Electricity, Heat, or Biofuels

Detailed Explanation

Source of Energy: Biomass represents stored solar energy captured through plant photosynthesis. Agricultural residues like rice husks, sugarcane bagasse, wood chips, animal manure, and dedicated energy crops contain chemical energy bonds that can be released through various conversion processes.

Conversion Process: Biomass conversion follows multiple pathways depending on feedstock characteristics. Thermochemical processes like direct combustion burn biomass to produce heat for steam generation, while gasification produces combustible syngas at high temperatures with controlled oxygen. Biochemical routes employ microorganisms: anaerobic digestion breaks down organic matter producing methane-rich biogas, while fermentation converts sugars into ethanol. Each pathway transforms biomass's chemical energy into usable forms.

Output & Utilization: Systems generate electricity through steam or gas turbines, thermal energy for industrial processes or district heating, and transportation biofuels like ethanol and biodiesel. Digestate and ash serve as nutrient-rich fertilizers.

Real-World Application: Sugar mills in Maharashtra utilize bagasse for cogeneration, simultaneously producing sugar and electricity. Biogas plants in rural Punjab convert cattle waste into cooking fuel and organic fertilizer, demonstrating biomass energy's waste-to-wealth potential while addressing energy access and agricultural waste management challenges sustainably.

‚Üì

4. Steam Turbine

High-pressure steam drives turbine blades, converting thermal energy to mechanical rotation

‚Üì

5. Generator

Turbine shaft rotates generator producing electrical energy via electromagnetic induction

‚Üì

6. Injection Well System

Condensed water reinjected into reservoir for sustainability; electricity distributed to grid

Detailed Explanation

Source of Energy: Geothermal energy derives from Earth's molten core, maintained by radioactive element decay and residual planetary formation heat. This thermal energy heats underground rock formations and aquifers, creating geothermal reservoirs with temperatures reaching 150-350¬∞C at accessible depths in tectonically active regions.

Conversion Process: Three main technologies exist: Dry steam plants directly use steam from reservoirs to drive turbines. Flash steam systems depressurize hot water, producing steam through rapid vaporization. Binary cycle plants transfer heat from moderate-temperature fluids to a secondary working fluid with lower boiling point, which then vaporizes to drive turbines. All systems convert geothermal thermal energy through steam turbines into mechanical rotation, subsequently transformed into electricity by generators.

Output & Utilization: Geothermal plants provide baseload electricity with capacity factors exceeding 90%, producing 5-100 MW per plant. Direct-use applications include district heating, greenhouse agriculture, and industrial processes utilizing the thermal energy without conversion.

Real-World Application: Iceland generates 25% of its electricity from geothermal sources, with Reykjavik's district heating serving 95% of buildings. In India, Puga Valley in Ladakh shows geothermal potential. The Philippines' Malitbog power plant demonstrates reliable baseload power generation in volcanic regions, providing consistent renewable energy unaffected by weather variations.
